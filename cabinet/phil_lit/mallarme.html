This virginal long-living lovely day
will it tear from us with a wing's wild blow
the lost hard lake haunted beneath the snow
by clear ice-flights that never flew away!

A swan of old remembers it is he
superb but strives to break free woebegone
for having left unsung the territory
to live when sterile winter's tedium shone

His neck will shake off this white throe that space
has forced the bird denying it to face,
but not the horror of Earth that traps its wings.

Poésies
read in translation

I found Mallarmé very difficult to understand, and had to re-read stanzas many many times to even grasp their concept. He has, to the point where it becomes abrasive, a dedication to the effect over the object. This is perhaps not a particularly exciting trait given where we are at our point in history several phases past the onset of modernism, but he makes all the trailing and fading about the object uniquely rigid in the straitjacket of his form, which is semi-classical; that, with his preoccupation with something like the Platonic Ideal, gave to me something entirely different than the psychological and subjective effect of "typical" modernism, as far as there is such a thing. The introduction to the collection I was reading talks of how his writing is particularly musical—maybe… maybe in some composerly or baroque way (I shy away from associating him with the lushness of Debussy, with whom he is often compared to, or just melodiousness in general)—but more than that, even when the content is dreamy and luminous and the flute literally makes the air thick, I think, rather, that his writing maintains something of the austerity of a textile.
